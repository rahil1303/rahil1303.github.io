## On research and why I am here

A PhD, to me, is not a credential. It is a way of thinking that you commit to for years, and if you are lucky, for a career. I want that career. Teaching has made that clearer than anything else: there is something about explaining a difficult idea to someone, watching them push back, and arriving somewhere neither of you expected, that I find more satisfying than most things. I have done it across six courses over two years, and I keep signing up.

I am not someone who agrees to agree. I think the best ideas in research come out of disagreement, not consensus. When a result does not sit right with me or an assumption feels shaky, I say so. I would rather have a difficult conversation that sharpens an idea than stay quiet and let something weak pass. I think that instinct is a feature, not a bug, in research environments where the work actually matters.

The part nobody tells you about research is where the insight actually happens. Sometimes it clicks at your desk, which is its own kind of satisfaction. But most of the time, it does not. It lives in the back of your head while you are cooking, or walking, or mid-conversation about something completely unrelated, and then it arrives. Those moments, the ones you cannot engineer or schedule, are why I do what I do. I have learned to trust the process enough to let the problem sit.

I am a foodie in the most committed sense: I will go out of my way for a good meal, and I have strong opinions about most cuisines. I play a lot of sports and take fitness seriously. I also dance, which surprises people who only know me from a seminar room.

I read about conspiracy theories, not because I believe them, but because of what they reveal about how people construct meaning from incomplete information, which is, when you think about it, not entirely unlike what researchers do.

I watch a lot of films and shows, and I am almost always more interested in character than plot. Right now I am deep in **House of the Dragon** and rewatching **Game of Thrones** for the character arcs: the redemptions, the falls, the people who had every reason to be good and chose not to be. That kind of writing stays with me.
