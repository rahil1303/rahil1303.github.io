Hi, I’m Rahil. I think of myself as a systems researcher. I am interested in why systems and protocols are shaped the way they are, and what their architectural choices enable, constrain, prevent, or imply.

My interests broadly span three connected areas. The first is systems and protocol design—the architectural logic behind why particular designs succeed, where they break down, and why one approach may be preferable to another. The second is reasoning about trade-offs under constraints, particularly where security, privacy, scalability, and efficiency exist in tension rather than in isolation. The third is the sociotechnical impact of systems: what happens when technical architecture meets human judgement, organisational practice, and real-world decision-making.

I approach these questions by building and evaluating systems. I am particularly drawn to work that moves between conceptual ideas, implementation, and empirical analysis: translating requirements into system designs, examining the assumptions behind them, and understanding how their behaviour changes under practical constraints.

I recently completed an MSc in Computer Science at Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam and the University of Amsterdam, with a specialisation in Big Data Engineering. My thesis research at CWI Amsterdam focused on the implementation and empirical comparison of oblivious protocols for privacy-preserving computation. My broader work has explored trustworthy machine learning, explainability, graph-derived analysis, and the evaluation of LLM-based systems.

My master’s thesis at CWI Amsterdam introduced Oblivious Bloom Filter Insertion (OBFI), a protocol for constructing encrypted Bloom filters while concealing insertion access patterns. I implemented OBFI as a distributed system and evaluated its computational and communication trade-offs against an alternative privacy-preserving protocol that solves the same problem. 

Alongside my thesis, I have taken on projects across explainable machine learning, NLP and computational social science, energy-aware systems, fraud detection, AI Agentic systems, and LLM evaluation. I see these projects as opportunities to enter unfamiliar fields and understand their problems from the inside. I learn best by engaging with the literature, building a working system, and examining its behaviour through empirical evaluation.

Before and alongside my research, I worked across enterprise data systems and university teaching. These experiences shaped my interest in interdisciplinary problems where reliable technical systems, human judgement, organisational requirements, and decision-making come together. They also taught me to communicate technical choices across different levels of expertise and to treat operational constraints as part of the research problem.
